Why Your Business Should Care About Accessibility?

Dollars and Sense, That's Why.

You may not realize it but accessibility is not just for people with disabilities but for everyone and anyone who uses the internet, and that accessibility should be a baked in part of the design process from day one. And, although it is more challenging to remediate and implement web accessibility standards after the fact, it's far from impossible and it can be done in phases, as your time and budget permit. By making websites and products accessible, not only do we help improve the quality of life for may disabled people but, we also potentially increase our reach and revenue while simultaneously reducing operational costs and financial risks owing to potential regulatory compliance litigation. Did you know the total disposable income for U.S. adults with disabilities is about $490 billion a year, can your business afford to ignore customers with such great spending power?

About 20% of Americans today currently live with some form of disability and 15% of the world's' population are disabled, that's more than 1 billion people worldwide. At some point, most of us likely will face some type of temporary, situational or permanent disability ourselves, whether it stems from developing an illness, experiencing injury, or finding your motion severely limited in situations such as caring for an infant with one hand and trying to use the other to navigate daily routines...

Even if you aren't disabled, you likely know someone who is. Disabilities include a vast array of conditions and symptoms from varying categories such as visual, hearing, mobility, speech, cognitive, and neurological. The symptoms that affect a persons navigation of digital products come in many forms and can affect you in many ways like needing to wear glasses, color blindness, a broken arm, age related symptoms such as low vision, neuropathy (nerve pain), learning disabilities, stammering or a heavy accent, neurodiversity like Dyslexia, ADHD or Autism, and even neurological conditions like Parkinson's, Fibromyalgia, or Epilepsy. and digital worlds more accessible.

How many of of you have used Siri or other voice assistants to make a call or send a text message? What about using an electric toothbrush, bendable straw, or a kitchen utensil with a rubber grip on it? Or how about listened to an audio book or used a typewriter or keyboard? If you can answer yes to any of these questions then you have used assisitive technology either for convenience or because of need. In fact all of the mentioned products and many of the technologies that make our lives convenient were actually developed as assistive technology for people with disabilities. So, as you can see including the disabled user and disabled employees in your design process is not only the right thing to do but it's also good for business and it drives innovation and has increase profits for many companies.

In the United States, 35 percent of the disabled population is of working age (ages 16–65) and earns income through employment or supplemental support benefits and have significant spending power. The total disposable income for U.S. adults with disabilities is about $490 billion, which is comparable to other significant market segments. Discretionary income or the income left after after deducting taxes, other mandatory charges, and spending on necessities, such as food and housing, for working-age people with disabilities is about $21 billion, which is greater than that of the African-American ($3 billion) and Hispanic ($16 billion) market segments, combined. Can your business afford to ignore disabled customers? In order to understanding and serving these consumers may require changes to both your business and industry practices. The first step towards these changes is to hire and retaining people with disabilities, involving them in the development and production of products and services, and including adults with disabilities in advertising and marketing efforts. All of these are essential strategies for accessing this market.

Consumers are becoming increasingly inclusive each year and are more likely to support businesses that are inclusive and while inclusive consumers tend to be younger, female, and more racially diverse, they include men and women spanning diverse ethnic backgrounds, income levels, and age groups. Given this representation, the inclusive consumer holds the unique power to influence all demographic groups. Two out of three Americans say that social values now shape their shopping choices and, they base their shopping choices less on traditional advertising and more on social media, friends’ recommendations. So, being at the forefront of accessibility and inclusion can help you reach a wider audience and increase your revenue and brand loyalty while reshaping the spaces we occupy and the products we use.

As someone who uses Assistive Technology daily I know first hand that assistive technology and the internet don't always play nice. In fact, many websites and applications are not designed with accessibility in mind at all or they use widgets or overlays such as the ones found on e-commerce content management system sites that claim to make your site fully accessible when, spoiler alert...they do not. Many accessibility issues can't be fixed or even tested with automated testing software. While automated testing software can be a basic helpful first step in identifying some issues, it can't identify many of the most common issues that disabled customers experience. And, in addition to missing many errors, they can also create new ones, for example by incorrectly describing an image in it's alt text read by screen readers to describe an image. This highlights the importance of human user testing done by real people with disabilities so you can get a clear picture of your product or website’s issues that need remediation to ensure a better user experience.

Inaccessible websites largely prevent people who are blind or have low vision from accessing goods and services. This isn't a trivial matter when you think about how many services and products for both consumers and employees operate partly or primarily online or through apps, it inhibits access to everything from shopping online to communications, to applying for jobs, accessing health benefits, and the ability to search and use databases like those in online banking, online bill payment, cloud storage, streaming services, gaming, social media, weather prediction, the news, Government Organizations, and many other vital services that we use every day without a thought and are indispensable.

Without being able to access your product, people with disabilities are at a disadvantage in many ways. For example, This can make it difficult or even impossible for people to access the information they need for their daily lives. such as purchasing groceries or household goods, or even booking an appointment. And, from a company or business owners standpoint, if your products are not accessible then you are missing out on a large portion of the population that could potentially be your customers and would gladly prefer to patronize brands, websites, and products that not only think of them but were designed with them in mind, and yes, we can tell when that is the case. I'll let you in on a little secret, when disabled people find a product or service that makes our lives easier we are loyal customers for life. But, on that same note, when the opposite is true we will not only never use your product or service again but we will also tell everyone we know about our experience.

A 2019 report by accessibility software company Deque Systems and Nucleus Research showed that about 70 percent of businesses in e-commerce, news and information, and government categories had significant accessibility issues for people who are visually impaired, and that is just a single type of disability surveyed. Internet users who are blind abandoned two Internet transactions per month because of website inaccessibility and called a company's customer service department once a week to navigate around the accessibilities, the report found. Not only are there the economic costs of loss of business and potential transactions but there are also increased operational costs and loss of time and productivity as well as an increase in labor costs to think about when addressing customer service issues that are preventable by making your products and website accessible.

What do Target, Amazon, Netlix, Burger King, Blue Apron, Harvard University, H&R Block, Winn-Dixie Supermarkets, and Beyonce Knowles have in common? They have all been sued under Title III of the ADA for website Accessibility compliance issues. Title III ADA lawsuits and settlements can affect companies of all sizes and across a wide range of industries. If you, like many people are under the mistaken impression that the ADA only applies to physical places of business, you are not alone. However, Title III of the Americans with Disabilities Act has been interpreted by many courts to include websites as places legally required to provide public accommodation. Because of this, numerous entities – from sole proprietors and small businesses to the biggest corporations – have received demand letters or had lawsuits filed against them. And, you should be aware of accessibility when it comes to partnerships and services you use as well.

In 2019, a class-action lawsuit was filed against Beyonce Knowles’ company, Park Entertainment, because the website that sold concert tickets and other goods and services related to Beyonce’s music was missing several fundamentals of website accessibility. This high-profile Title III ADA lawsuit settlement put the spotlight on some of the most fundamental elements of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG). Think your business is too small to be sued for non-compliance? Think again, companies with revenue of less than $50 million were the targets of two-thirds of digital-accessibility lawsuits in 2021, a shift from a year earlier, when the share was less than half, according to a report from UsableNet.

As consumers' became reliant on e-commerce and other digital experiences that sprang forth out of the Covid-19 pandemic, problems with digital accessibility were exposed and there has been a heightened awareness surrounding the issues and how to solve them since then. As a result, lawsuits alleging violations of the Americans with Disabilities Act have surged and that trend is expected to continue. And, as a result many countries have now ratified their non-discrimination and digital accessibility laws to include the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) as a standard to follow in making websites and applications accessible to people with disabilities. Most international web accessibility laws now demand websites and applications are perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust enough to work for people with disabilities too.

Once your website and products are accessible the responsibility does not end there. You must also maintain your accessibility efforts long-term. No software, service, or human being can instantly identify and fix, all of your digital accessibility problems permanently. Just as technology and the digital landscape are ever-evolving, so are your human consumers and users. True accessibility requires a three pronged approach that includes long-term, comprehensive digital accessibility testing, remediation, and maintenance program at regular intervals and when changes are made to your site and products.

Does your business use digital pdfs, powerpoints, or other documents on your website or duringthe course of business? If so, you should be aware that those are also required to be accessible. This includes documents that are posted on websites, shared via email, or distributed to customers.
